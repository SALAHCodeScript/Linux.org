			    Preference
------------------------------------------------------------------
The fact that you like something or someone more than another 
thing or person
------------------------------------------------------------------
			     Prefer

Imagine that you are at a restaurant for breakfast. You want 
something to drink, but you are not sure what, When the server 
walks over to your table, he says:
	Let me help, Do you prefer coffee or juice?
The answer:
	I prefer juice to coffee

There's three different situation:
with noun, gerund or to (infinitive)

With noun: Prefer + Noun - For A Habit
Example:
	I prefer football

With gerund: Prefer + Gerund - For Liking Something
Example:
	I prefer playing football to watching football

With noun: Prefer + To
Example:
	I prefer to eat healthy food rather than fast food

You just told the server about a general preference, The server 
then makes a few suggestions about the teas available

When we are given choices, there are special phrases we use to 
say which thing we like more than another, We call these choices 
“preferences”

In our program today, we will show you how to use general and 
specific preferences, We have different phrases for each

Example:
	I prefer juice to coffee
	I prefer juice over coffee

As you might know, in English, when we answer a question, we do 
not usually answer with full wording, If asked about a preference,
you can simply name the preference, like this:

Example:
	I prefer tea

Notice that the word “or” is used in the question form, In the 
answer, we use the preposition “to” or “over”

Example:
	Do you prefer tea or coffee?

		          Would prefer
Just like “would rather,” we can use “would prefer” to talk 
about specific preferences, But, to Americans, “would prefer”
sounds more formal and we do not use it often in speaking. Here
is an example of a question and answer:
	Would you prefer to go out or stay home?
The answer:
	I’d prefer to go out

There's two different situation, in the present and in the past
And with subject changes

in the present with one subject: Would prefer + to + verb
Normal Example:
	I would prefer to go out than stay home
	I’d prefer to go out than stay home
Question Example:
	Would you prefer to go out or stay home?

in the present with two subject: Would prefer + sub2 + to + verb
Normal Example:
	I would prefer you to go out than stay home
	I’d prefer you to go out than stay home
Question Example:
	Would I prefer to go went out or stay home?

in the past/regret with one subject:
Would have preferred + to + verb
Normal Example:
	I would have preferred to go out than stay home
	I’d have preferred to go out than stay home
	I would’ve preferred to go out than stay home
Question Example:
	Would you have preferred to go out or stay home?

in the past/regret with two subject:
Would have preferred + sub2 + to + verb
Normal Example:
	I would have preferred you to go out than stay home
	I’d have preferred you to go out than stay home
	I would’ve preferred you to go out than stay home
Question Example:
	Would I have preferred you to go out or stay home?

Preference for the actions of another person:
Would prefer it + if + sub2 + past simple
Normal Example:
	I would prefer it if you cooked
	I’d prefer it if you cooked
Question Example:
	Would I prefer it if you cooked?

Note that, when Americans do use “would prefer,” we usually only
name the preference, as in the example

However, when the answer mentions both choices, Americans 
usually say it this way:
	I’d prefer to go out than stay home
__________________________________________________________________
		          Would rather

A specific preference is something you like or want more at a 
given point in time, either right now or in the future

We use the expressions “would prefer” and “would rather” to 
talk about such things, These phrases have the same meaning

For both phrases, we almost always use the short form of the 
word “would” with a personal pronoun, We call the shortened 
form a “contraction”, For example, the contraction of “I would” 
is “I’d”, When "would" is contracted, it looks and sounds just 
like the letter “d”

There's two different situation, in the present and in the past
And with subject changes

in the present with one subject: Would rather + verb
Normal Example:
	I would rather go out than stay home
	I’d rather go out than stay home
Question Example:
	Would you rather go out or stay home?

in the present with two subject:
Would rather + sub2 + past simple
Normal Example:
	I would rather you went out than stay home
	I’d rather you went out than stay home
Question Example:
	Would I rather you went out or stay home?

in the past/regret with one subject:
Would rather + present perfect infinitive
Normal Example:
	I would rather have gone out than stay home
	I’d rather have gone out than stay home
Question Example:
	Would you rather have gone out or stay home?

in the past/regret with two subject:
Would rather + sub2 + past perfect infinitive
Normal Example:
	I would rather you had gone out than stay home
	I’d rather you had gone out than stay home
Question Example:
	Would I rather you’d gone out or stay home?

Again, we usually only name the preference in responses
Example:
	I’d rather go out

And, if there is only one main verb after “would rather,” we 
do not say it two times, Here is an example:
	Would they rather study French or English?
And the answer:
	They’d rather study English

Notice that the verbs “go,” “stay” and “study” are in simple 
form – the basic form without “to” before it or any letters at 
the end, After “would rather,” we always use the simple form of 
verbs
__________________________________________________________________
		           Like better

But Americans are more likely to use the phrase “like…better” 
when talking with others. We use the word “than” after this 
phrase, Here is how we do that in a question and answer:
	Do you like tea or coffee better?
The answer:
	I like tea better than coffee

Again, when answering, we usually only name the preference:
	I like tea better

Notice that, in question form, the word “better” comes at the 
end of the question
